The data visualized in this graph were abstracted from a data set comprising reported incidents of crime in the city of Chicago, Illinois from 2001 to present downloaded from data.gov on 4/27/18. The 6,584,682 records provide information regarding the date, location, and classification of the reported crime and whether or not an arrest was made. The csv was read into a Python Pandas dataframe for manipulation.

This visualization specifically references those records having an Illinois Uniform Crime Reporting classification of '0820' which corresponds to thefts of money or property valued at less than $500, the most commonly reported crime in this data set, and data for the current year are excluded. The interactive storytelling visualization was generated using the Python Bokeh library. Annually aggregated counts are shown as a stacked bar plot with the total number of reported incidents resulting in an arrest at the base of the stack and those not resulting in an arrest stacked on top. Two distinct colors are used to clearly differentiate arrests from non-arrests, and bar fill is desaturated by reducing alpha. The x-axis corresponds to date and the y-axis (left) corresponds to count.

The same data were aggregated with finer granularity (monthly) and plotted as a line plot with overlaid circle glyphs at each data point. The line plot shares the same x-axis as the stacked bar chart, but given the difference in data ranges, a left y-axis is drawn at an appropriate scale for the monthly data.

Interactive tools which can be toggled on and off are positioned to the right of the plot and include a pan tool, wheel zoom, and a reset to restore the plot to its original appearance. Additionally, there are two hover tools that are toggled on by default and give data values used to generate the figure when the cursor is positioned at the same x value as the glyph of interest. Positioning the bar chart hover tool at the same x-value as one of the bars causes that bar to become saturated (increased alpha), and positioning the line plot hover tool at the same x-value as one of the circle glyphs results in a change in the fill color to red so that selected glyphs can be easily identified.

From the stacked bar chart we see a gradual reduction in the number of reported incidents of theft over the first half of 00 decade (data for the year 2000 itself is missing) with values from 2005 on being less than half the value in 2001. We also see a reduction in the percent of those reported incidents resulting in an arrest from a high value (over the data shown) of 20% in 2001 to values ranging from about 3-6% from 2003 on. Interestingly, from the monthly data we see a seasonality to thievery with peaks occurring during the warmest months of the year and troughs during the winter months. Either the crooks have an aversion to Chicago winters, or, more likely, have a reduced pedestrian population to pickpocket.
